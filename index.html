Part 1 - Good Visualizations:

Overrepresentation/Underrepresentation in the Electoral College

Image Source

Who is the audience? The general population of the United States and those who are interested in learning more about the electoral college and its merits for use in elections.

What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer? Seeks to address whether each state has been overrepresented or underrepresented in the electoral college based on overall population between 1960 and 2015. The overall message is that less populous states are generally more overrepresented and more populous states are usually underrepresented. Further, states usually tend to be underrepresented by a much larger margin than those who are overrepresented. Lastly, each state appears to have generally remained either overrepresented or underrepresented between 1960 and 2016, with very few states ever crossing over between the two.

What data is encoded in the visualization? Information as to whether each state is overrepresented or underrepresented in the electoral college based on population from the year 1960 to 2016 and exactly how over/underrepresented that they are.

How does the visualization encode the data?

  • Marks: Squares/Area: encodes the amount of over/underrepresentation in the electoral college
  • Channels: Position: location of each state in chart encodes its location on the map of the United States, Length: length of vertical bars encodes the difference in percentage points between share of population and share of electoral votes to show over/underrepresentation in the electoral college, Color: blue signifies overrepresentation, orange signifies underrepresentation

What tasks do readers perform on the visualization? Readers can see whether a particular state has been over or underrepresented in the electoral college in general from 1960-2016, identifying extremes to see which states are the most over/underrepresented. Further, readers can see how the population and representation in the electoral college of each state has changed over time by comparing the distance from the center line over time. By comparing the amount of over and underrepresentation in a particular state with that state’s general voting habits, a reader can determine what kind of impact that representation would have on an election.

How are the five principles applied to this visualization?

  • Truthful: The chart uses actual census data and the actual number of electoral college votes, disclosing its sources to maintain truthfulness.
  • Functional: The chart is very readable and not over-cluttered, making it easy to tell whether a given state has been over or underrepresented with just a quick glance. Sizes of the areas under/over the line allows a reader to easily answer questions about the representation of a given state over time.
  • Beautiful: The chart is very elegantly designed in a simple, minimal, and clean way, showing only the data, and not cluttering the chart with anything else. The colors contrast well with each other, and the layout is nice to look at since it mimics a map of the United States.
  • Insightful: This visualization is helpful since it helps a reader to analyze the effectiveness of the electoral college based on real data. The addition of the captions relating the population of a given state and their over/underrepresented statuses allows the reader to make meaningful connections and conclusions about how population plays into electoral college dynamics.
  • Enlightening: This chart reveals how more populous states are more likely to be underrepresented in the electoral college than more populous states. By comparing the data with a states’ voting habits, a reader is able to further explore how representation can impact the outcome of an election. With this, we can better evaluate whether the electoral college, which plays a major role in the political workings of our country, is a system that is worth preserving.

Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad? This visualization is good because it presents important, factual data in a way that is easy to read and understand. It provides insight into the workings of the electoral college, which is very important to the foundation of this country. Each of the five principles of visualization are well-addressed in this visualization, making it effective overall.

Corona Virus Riskiest Activities

Image Source

Who is the audience? The audience for this visualization is those affected by the corona virus pandemic, which is the general population. This was published in the New York times and is specifically geared towards those who are reentering social scenes as Covid restrictions loosen. Additionally, the information in this graphic is also available in German, Portuguese, Serbian and Spanish.

What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer? It informs the viewer of the level of risk different activities have in relation to contracting the Covid 19 pandemic. It sorts activities by risk level and also gives the viewer additional risk factors to be aware of.

What data is encoded in the visualization? The data encoded in the visualization is on different settings and their associated risk factor for contracting Covid 19, as well as different factors that contribute to overall risk level.

How does the visualization encode the data?

  • Marks: Circles: encodes the activities associated with differnet levels of risk for contracting Covid
  • Channels: Position: location of circle in the graph signifies its risk level, with those having coordinates closest to (0,0) having the lowest risk, Area: circles increase slightly in size as they increase in risk level, Color: yellow signifies low risk, orange signifies medium risk and red signifies high risk.

What tasks do readers perform on the visualization? The reader is able to identify activities that are more and less risky in association to contracting Covid 19. For example, a reader can quickly recognize that tennis, outdoor exercise and takeout food are some of the least risky activities whereas going to a night club is a slight outlier in the sense that it is the riskiest activity which is demonstrated by the fact it is the biggest circle and the darkest shade of red on the visualization.

How are the five principles applied to this visualization?

  • Truthful: This visualization is truthful because lists its data sources: New York times, Reuters, NPR, SF Gate & others, and provides links to the data. It also states that its findings are according to 500+ epidemiologists and health professionals.
  • Functional: This visualization was easy to read and understand without being too cluttered. It used accurate color and position to represent riskiness and it supports the meaningful task which is to inform viewers of risk level associated with different activities as well as risk factors to consider.
  • Beautiful: The color scheme is attractive as well as easy to understand with yellow being low risk and red being high risk, the colors emulate an understanding of color coding many already have, such as with stop lights where yellow is slow and red is stop. Additionally, the font is easy to read and the use of black to label the activities is pleasant to look at.
  • Insightful: the visualization confirmed my thoughts about different activities and the Covid risk associated with them, and also revealed some surprises such as church being a high-risk activity due to the singing and that walking in a busy downtown is actually considered a low risk activity. The ability to compare different activities’ risks allows the viewer to compare activities. For some activities, the visualization provided additional details on why it is categorized the way it is, such as the beach being low risk, but only if you are able to social distance.
  • Enlightening: This visualization went beyond just showcasing which are the riskiest activities, it also provided details about specific categorizations as well as risk factors to consider. As a result, this visualization makes the reader question their behaviors and activities in this unprecedented time of the pandemic and their action’s effect on themselves and others.

Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad? This is a good visualization for multiple reasons. First, the visualization is visually appealing and easy to understand through use of color, positioning and clear labels. It also does more than just showcase which activities are risky – it tells you why for activities that are confusing and also lists risk factors to consider that contribute to the riskiness of each activity. The visual gives the viewer a better understanding of why an activity is risky. This visualization is also very useful. In a time when the country is reopening but the virus still looms large, having an understanding of the risk associated with different activities is very helpful.

Best in Show: The Ultimate Data Dog

Image Source

Who is the audience? The target audience includes avid dog lovers, people looking to purchase or rescue a dog and people that are casually interested in the variation between dog breeds.

What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer? This visualization conveys how different breeds of dogs compare against eachother. Questions about the differneces in popularity of dog breeds and the overall ranking of dog breed based on intelligence, costs, longevity, grooming, ailments and appetite are answered through the visualization.

What data is encoded in the visualization? The data encoded in the visualization is the popularity or unpopularity of each dog breeed as well as the intelligence, costs, longevity, grooming, ailments and appetite of the dog breeds.

How does the visualization encode the data?

  • Marks: Colored silhouettes of each different dog breed encode the type of dog breed as well as the size of each dog breed. The orientation of the mark also encodes how intelligent the dog is.
  • Channels: The positioning of the dog on the chart encodes how popular the dog is as well as the overall ranking of the dog relative to other dogs. The y-axis encodes popularity while the x-axis encodes the ranking of the dog.

What tasks do readers perform on the visualization? Readers look at each dog breed and can compare the position on the y-axis of each dog breed as well as the x-axis. It is natural to move through each quadrant and consider the labels of "Inexplicably Overrated", "Hot Dogs!", "The Rightly Ignored" and "Overlooked Treasures". Readers also take in the size of the dog and the orientation of the dog to determine how big and how intelligent the dog is.

How are the five principles applied to this visualization?

  • Truthful: The data is truthful as it pulls from reputable sources such as the American Kennel Club and lists each source that was used. The visualization also includes a link to the raw data so the reader can look at each aspect of what went into comparing the overall rank and popularity.
  • Functional: The visualization is easy to read and follow, especially with the help of dividing the data into four different quadrants with labels. The vizualization becomes slightly cluttered in some areas but with the help of labels on each mark no information is lost.
  • Beautiful: The visualization uses intriguing silhouettes of the actual dogs and a color scheme that is visually appealing. The font choices are clean and playful, as well as the marks and data keys making it fun to read.
  • Insightful: The visualization provides insight into how the dog breeds rank against each other in terms of popularity and overall ease to care for. It would be especially insightful to someone who is looking to purchase a dog.
  • Enlightening: The data in the visualization is enlightening especially in terms of showcasing dogs that are extremely popular with a low overall rankkng in terms of intelligence, costs, longevity, grooming, ailments and appetite. Beyond this, the visualization is enlightening by employing an overall ranking of each dog breed using the various parameters listed.

Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad? This visualization excels at beauty and functionality. It is easy to determine which breeds are easy to care and which breeds may be overrated. The silhouettes of the dogs used as markers are very fun to look at it and makes the data easy to digest. On top of this the visualization is insightful and provides a great tool for those actively seeking a dog to purchase.

Part 2 - Bad Visualizations:

How Much Do You Spend on Groceries Every Week?

Image Source

Who is the audience? People interested in comparing the spending habits of different people and the percentages at which these spending habits occur.

What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer? This chart addresses the question of how much money people spend on their groceries each week on average. It shows the percentages of people who spend each amount of money on groceries in order to answer this question, visualizing the percentages by making the size of each square correspond to the relative size of the percentages.

What data is encoded in the visualization? The percentage of people who spend each amount of money on their groceries each week, on average.

How does the visualization encode the data?

  • Marks: Area: Encodes the percentage of people who spend a certain amount of money on groceries each week
  • Channels: Color: encodes the different amounts of money spent, Size: The size of each square corresponds with the percentage of people who spend that amount of money on groceries.

What tasks do readers perform on the visualization? Readers compare the sizes of each square in order to determine how many more people spend a certain amount of money on groceries than another amount. Even though this visualization makes it difficult to see, readers should be able to see the sort of bell-curve like trend in the data, with more people spending more middle-tier amounts of money than either of the extremes.

How are the five principles applied to this visualization?

  • Truthful: No source is given in this visualization, which makes it difficult to assess the truthfulness of the data itself. The way that the visualization itself is laid out makes it difficult to discern the truth as to how many people fall into each category.
  • Functional: This chart is very difficult to analyze due to the fact that the squares are overlapping. Because of this, for example, the “about $100” square looks very small since we can only see a small outline of the square before it is cut off by the “under $100” square, even though it has the second largest percentage.
  • Beautiful: Aesthetically, the chart is very clean and simple with a nice color scheme that gives good contrast between the squares.
  • Insightful: Even though it is difficult to see, this graph provides the insight that most people tend to spend a more average amount of money ($100-200) on groceries each week than either of the more extreme amounts (less than $100 or more than $200)
  • Enlightening: This chart is enlightening since it shows how most people’s spending habits lie in the middle of the spectrum, reflecting the fact that the majority of people tend to fall into the middle class and less into the lower and upper classes.

Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad? I think this visualization is bad because it prevents the data in a very confusing way due to the overlap between the squares. The data is distorted since some squares which are actually larger look smaller since they are mostly covered by another square. Also, this particular presentation makes it difficult to see the bell-curve like trend that defines the data. Also, the lack of labels on the visualization itself make the chart hard to analyze, as the reader has to continue looking back and forth between the key and the chart.

VISUALIZATION TITLE

Image Source

Who is the audience? The audience for this visualization is coffee and caffeine consumers, particularly those who are concerned about calories or the caffeine to calorie ratio.

What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer? This visualization tells the viewer the associated calorie and caffeine count of different drinks and foods, as well as compares what kind of physical exercise one could do to burn the calorie equivalent of certain foods and drinks.

What data is encoded in the visualization? Calories and caffeine amount of different foods and drinks are encoded in the visualization. In addition, the number of calories burnt by certain activities are included.

How does the visualization encode the data?

  • Marks: silhouette images: encoded the data of drinks and foods
  • Channels: postion: the postion on the plot indicates the amount of caffeine and calories in each item

What tasks do readers perform on the visualization? The reader of the visualization can compare the different calories and caffeine amount in different beverages and food options.

How are the five principles applied to this visualization?

  • Truthful: This visualization is not all truthful. Though it does disclose its sources, which are guardian data store, Starbucks and calorie-counter.com, the graphic’s title “the buzz vs the bulge” does nothing to imply that the coffee calorie which is being listed is based only off Starbucks’s coffee, which has more added sugar than other brands, and this nuance is not easily identifiable to the reader. Additionally, the x and y axis can be misleading. The center point to this graph is (150,150). It is confusing to understand the context of this in the graph and makes it more difficult to read the different calorie and caffeine associations with different objects. For example, energy drinks are very close to the x axis and from first glance one may assume that it’s calorie count is close to 0, however it is actually close to 200 calories. The axis labels are somewhat untruthful in the sense that it may distort the meaning of the data.
  • Functional: This visualization is not the most functional. The graph is somewhat cluttered with over 40 data points, and many of them are repeating the same drink in the different sizes or are foods that are not directly related to the topic of the visualization, such as big macs and fries. Additionally, there are many data points on the graph which are the same silhouette. For example, the large mocha Frappuccino is the same image as the large mocha Frappuccino with whipped cream and there is a coffee mug picture that is used to represent 7 different drinks. As a result, the pictures themselves are not very useful. The labels are very small and you have to zoom in to read them which makes it hard to understand the bigger picture. Contributing to the same problem of not being able to understand the big picture, the viewer is not able to see the whole visualization on one web page because it is so large and cluttered with data.
  • Beautiful: : The color scheme is good in this visualization and goes together. The brown hues also relate back to the central focus of the visualization which is coffee and more broadly caffeine.
  • Insightful: This visualization could be helpful if a person is trying to limit calorie intake while still consuming a large amount of caffeine. For example, the person would be able to see an iced coffee provides the highest amount of caffeine with the lowest number of calories.
  • Enlightening: This visualization is not very enlightening. Though it can help someone to understand how much caffeine and calories are in a particular food or drink, it is also not very easy to apply to coffee in general, since the data is only taken from Starbucks coffee. Additionally, it would be hard to use this to guide any life style changes since in some cases the visualization includes the size of the drink and in some cases, it doesn’t. Though the visualization is interesting to look at and think about it lacks practical use that could make it enlightening.

Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad? This visualization is a bad one. It is not entirely truthful, since the data source is misleading and does not tell the viewer the calories for coffee are only pulled from Starbucks. Additionally, the graph is cluttered and difficult to read. The x and y axis intersect at (150, 150) which adds to the confusion in reading it and the images for different coffees are repeated making them unhelpful in decoding the graph.

The Billion Dollar-o-Gram

Image Source

Who is the audience? The audience of this visualization is people that have a general understanding of the economy and are interested in it.

What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer? This visualization answers questions about where and how money in the US economy is being spent or earned.

What data is encoded in the visualization? Data encoded in the visualization includes where money in the economy is being spent and where it is coming from. It also encodes how much money is either being spent or earned.

How does the visualization encode the data?

  • Marks: Various sized colored blocks encode amounts of money being spent in the economy. The size of the blocks indicate how much money is being spent.
  • Channels: The color of each block indicates how the money is being spent or earned.

What tasks do readers perform on the visualization? Readers look at the blocks and consider the size of each block as well as use the data key and labels to consider how money is being spent or earned.

How are the five principles applied to this visualization?

  • Truthful: The visualization is somewhat truthful as it lists the New York Times, Guardian and BBC as its data sources. However, it contains no link to specific articles or data sourcs for verification of the data. The visualization is also somewhat misleading because it discloses that the size of the blocks are not exactly proportional but were morphed slightly to fit a block structure.
  • Functional: Functionality is where I think this visualization fails the most. It is difficult to read as it is not entirely clear what the visualization is conveying. The title is not specific and is not very insightful as to what the data is actually conveying. It is unclear if the data is reflecting the US economy or the global economy. Users must infer from the labels on the blocks to determine that the visualization is most likely reflecting the global economy. I think the categories indicating how money is being transferred is also fairly confusing. The two categories of earning and accumulating do not seem distinctly different to me and there is no specific definition of what they entail. Finally it is unclear why the creators chose to include the transactions that they did. Is the data included the top transactions over the last ten years? Or did the creators decide to include what they thought would be interesting?
  • Beautiful: This visualization is somewhat beautiful. The colors for the blocks are visually appealing and the way in which the blocks fit nicely into one big rectangle is nice.
  • Insightful: This visualization is somewhat insightful. It includes information on the top transactions of the economy and a short description for why each transaction took place. However, beyond this there is not much more depth as to when, why and for what period of time each transaction took place.
  • Enlightening: The visualization is generally enlightening as it provides readers with a better understanding of the relative costs and earnings of different aspects of our economy.

Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad? This visualization is bad because it lacks many important aspects of functionality that make it difficult to understand and digest. It is not clear what time period and area the data is representing and the data cannot be easily verified to be true.
